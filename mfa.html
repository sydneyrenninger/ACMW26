Multi-Factor Authentication

Add an extra layer of security so that even if someone gets your password, they still can’t get in.

What Is MFA?

Multi-factor authentication (MFA) means proving who you are with more than one type of proof. Instead of only entering a password, you also provide something you have (like your phone) or something you are (like your fingerprint). That way, a stolen or guessed password alone isn’t enough to access your account.

Why Use It?

Passwords can leak in breaches, get phished, or be guessed. MFA greatly reduces the risk: even if an attacker has your password, they usually can't complete the second step. Many services report that turning on MFA blocks almost all automated account takeovers.

Types of Factors

Something you know — a password or PIN. Something you have — your phone (SMS or app codes), a security key, or a badge. Something you are — fingerprint or face recognition. The strongest setup uses two different kinds (e.g., password + authenticator app or security key), not two of the same (e.g., two passwords).

Quick Tip

Prefer an authenticator app (e.g., Google Authenticator, Microsoft Authenticator) or a hardware security key over SMS codes when possible. SMS can be intercepted. Enable MFA on email, banking, and any account that holds sensitive data first.
